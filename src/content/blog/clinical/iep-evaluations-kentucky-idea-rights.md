When a school evaluation does not quite match what you know about your child — or when an IEP meeting is approaching and the existing testing feels thin — it is easy to feel stuck. You may not know what the school is required to provide, what rights you have as a parent, or what an outside **Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE)** could actually do for your child's educational plan.

This post covers what Kentucky families need to know about IEP-related evaluations, the federal protections that apply under the **Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA)**, and how Twilight Psychology can support school advocacy from the evaluation through the IEP meeting itself.

## What is an IEP evaluation?

An Individualized Education Program (IEP) is a legally binding document that outlines the special education services and accommodations a school district is required to provide to a child with a qualifying disability. Before an IEP can be created or updated, the school must conduct a comprehensive evaluation to determine whether the child qualifies for services and what those services should be.

The school's evaluation typically includes cognitive testing, academic achievement measures, and sometimes behavioral or adaptive rating scales. However, school evaluations are conducted by district employees under system constraints — limited budgets, eligibility-focused criteria, and the pressure to keep services conservative. The result is often a report that describes what the school can offer rather than what the child actually needs.

A **psychoeducational evaluation** completed by an independent clinician looks at the full picture: cognitive and intellectual profile, academic achievement, processing speed and working memory, attention and executive functioning, and social-emotional functioning. The goal is clinical accuracy, not eligibility gatekeeping.

## Your rights under IDEA — independent educational evaluations

Federal law gives parents of children with disabilities specific protections when it comes to evaluation. One of the most important is the right to an **Independent Educational Evaluation (IEE)**.

Under **34 CFR § 300.502**, parents who disagree with their school district's evaluation have the right to request an IEE conducted by a qualified examiner who is not employed by the school district. That independence matters — an outside clinician has no stake in the outcome and no institutional interest in limiting services.

If you request an IEE at public expense, the school district must, without unnecessary delay, either:

- **Fund the independent evaluation** at no cost to you, or
- **File a due process hearing** to defend the adequacy of their own assessment.

The school district cannot simply deny the request. They must respond — and if they choose to defend their own evaluation, that triggers a formal hearing process. In practice, many families find that requesting an IEE brings the school system to the table in a way that a parent's verbal concerns alone cannot.

You are entitled to one publicly funded IEE each time the school conducts an evaluation with which you disagree. If the school's hearing establishes that their evaluation was appropriate, you still have the right to an IEE — just not at public expense.

## When to consider requesting an independent evaluation

Not every disagreement with a school evaluation rises to the level of needing an IEE. Here are situations where an independent evaluation tends to make the most clinical and practical difference:

**The school evaluation missed something you have seen at home or in other settings.** A district evaluation often focuses narrowly on academic performance. It may not capture attention difficulties that emerge in unstructured settings, processing differences that only show up under timed conditions, or emotional regulation concerns that the child masks at school.

**Your child is twice-exceptional.** Gifted children with learning disabilities or ADHD are among the most commonly under-served in public education. High cognitive ability can mask academic struggles, and a school evaluation may conclude that a gifted child does not qualify for services because their performance is grade-level — even when it is significantly below their own potential. An independent evaluation can document that discrepancy and make the case for support.

**The IEP does not reflect the child's actual needs.** If the goals seem generic, the accommodations feel inadequate, or the placement decisions do not align with what you know about your child, a more comprehensive evaluation can provide the documentation needed to push for meaningful change.

**You are preparing for a due process hearing or mediation.** Independent evaluations carry significant weight in formal dispute resolution. Under IDEA, an IEE that meets the school's criteria must be considered in placement and service decisions and may be presented as evidence in a due process hearing.

**The school evaluation is taking too long.** School districts are required to complete initial evaluations within 60 calendar days of receiving parental consent (in Kentucky, governed by state timelines under **707 KAR 1:320**). If timelines have been missed or the process has stalled, a private evaluation can move faster — self-pay evaluations at Twilight Psychology are typically completed and reported within **seven days of the final testing session**.

## What Twilight Psychology evaluates

An IEP-focused psychoeducational evaluation at Twilight Psychology typically assesses:

- **Cognitive and intellectual ability** — reasoning, fluid and crystallized intelligence, and the full profile of cognitive strengths and weaknesses
- **Academic achievement** — reading fluency, decoding, comprehension, written expression, and math skills
- **Processing speed and working memory** — measures that matter significantly for classroom performance and are often underassessed in school evaluations
- **Language and phonological processing** — especially relevant when dyslexia or reading disability is a question
- **Attention and executive functioning** — including measures relevant to ADHD screening and the functional impact on learning
- **Social-emotional and behavioral functioning** — ratings and clinical interview to capture the emotional component of learning difficulties

The report is written to be used — not just filed. It includes specific IEP goal language, accommodation recommendations written for school teams, and findings explained in plain language for parents who need to advocate effectively at the table.

## A note on confidentiality and your child's records

Under IDEA and the **Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA)**, you have the right to review all education records related to your child — including every evaluation report, IEP document, progress note, and eligibility determination — before any IEP meeting. You also have the right to request corrections to records you believe are inaccurate or misleading.

An independent evaluation completed by Twilight Psychology is your property. You control who receives it. You can choose to share it with the school district, present it in a due process hearing, or keep it for your own records.
