Twilight Psychology recently added a CAT-Q-based masking feature to help people reflect on how much energy they spend adapting, rehearsing, monitoring, or hiding parts of themselves in social situations. It is designed to be private, accessible, and practical. It is also important to be clear about what it is not: this tool is not medical advice, not a diagnosis, and not a substitute for a full psychological evaluation.

## Why we added a CAT-Q-based masking feature

Many adults who explore ADHD or autism later in life describe the same pattern: other people see them as capable, calm, or socially polished, while privately they feel exhausted, overprepared, or disconnected from their natural way of interacting. A masking tool can help put language around that experience.

Our goal with this feature is not to label someone after a few minutes online. The goal is to offer a structured way to notice patterns that are often hard to describe, especially if you have spent years automatically adapting to fit the room.

If you have not taken it yet, you can explore the [masking inventory](/resources/masking-inventory) on the site.

## What the CAT-Q is actually measuring

CAT-Q stands for Camouflaging Autistic Traits Questionnaire. In plain language, it looks at the strategies people use to get through social situations when being fully natural does not feel easy, safe, or effective.

That usually includes three broad kinds of patterns:

- Compensation: learning, practising, or rehearsing what to say and do
- Masking: actively hiding discomfort, differences, or traits other people might notice
- Assimilation: trying to fit in, appear typical, or avoid standing out

That matters because a masking score is not simply telling you whether you are shy, outgoing, awkward, or confident. It is describing how much effort may be going into appearing socially smooth or acceptable.

## How to think about your results without fixating on a number

The most useful way to read a masking result is not, "Is my score high enough?" It is, "Does this pattern sound like my real day-to-day experience, and does it come at a cost?"

In general, higher results can suggest that social situations require more conscious management. That may look like preparing scripts before conversations, adjusting facial expressions on purpose, watching how other people behave so you can copy the rhythm, or feeling like your public self is more polished than your private self.

More moderate results can reflect a mixed picture. Some people only mask in work settings, unfamiliar groups, or stressful environments. Others have certain strategies they use automatically, but not all the time.

Lower results do not automatically mean that nothing is going on. Sometimes they reflect a person who feels safer being direct, has found supportive environments, or does not rely heavily on camouflaging as their main way of coping. They can also reflect someone whose neurodivergent traits show up in ways that are less about masking and more about overload, attention, routines, sensory differences, or burnout.

## Why the pattern matters more than the formula

Two people can end up with similarly elevated results for very different reasons. One person may rely on rehearsal and learned social scripts. Another may spend more energy suppressing visible traits or trying not to seem different. The overall result matters, but the shape of the experience matters too.

That is why the best use of a CAT-Q-based tool is reflective rather than absolute. It can help you notice:

- whether your energy drops after social interactions
- whether conversations feel natural or heavily managed
- whether you monitor your face, voice, posture, or eye contact on purpose
- whether you feel pressure to appear more typical than you naturally are

These are clinically meaningful reflections, but they still need context.

## What a masking result cannot tell you

Even a strong result cannot tell you, on its own, whether you are autistic, ADHD, both, or neither. It also cannot separate masking from related experiences like trauma adaptation, social anxiety, chronic people-pleasing, or high-pressure environments where fitting in has felt necessary for safety.

A self-report tool also cannot replace developmental history, clinical interview, functional review, and formal testing. Those pieces matter because clinicians are not just asking whether you camouflage. They are also asking when it started, what it protects you from, how automatic it has become, and what happens when the effort is no longer sustainable.

## When to consider a fuller evaluation

If the masking feature resonates strongly, the next question is usually not whether you should keep retaking it. The better question is whether the pattern shows up across enough parts of your life to deserve a deeper look.

You may want a fuller evaluation if you regularly feel drained after everyday social contact, if your relationships or work depend on constant self-monitoring, or if you have a long history of being told you seem fine while privately feeling overwhelmed. In those cases, a comprehensive [psychological evaluation](/services/psychological-evaluations), [autism testing](/services/autism-testing), or [ADHD testing](/services/adhd-testing) can offer more useful answers than a screening result alone.

## A practical way to use this tool for self-reflection

The masking feature is most helpful when you treat it as a starting point for observation. After taking it, consider writing down:

- which social situations feel the most effortful
- which traits you hide or soften around other people
- how long recovery takes after social contact
- whether you rely on scripts, imitation, or performance to get through the day

Those notes can be useful if you later talk with a therapist or evaluator. They also help you move beyond a score and toward a more accurate picture of your actual lived experience.

## This is a self-reflection tool, not medical advice

Twilight Psychology's CAT-Q-based masking feature is meant to support self-reflection, not to provide medical advice. Online tools can help you notice patterns, ask better questions, and decide whether you want professional support. They cannot diagnose you and should not be treated as a final answer.

If you want to explore your own pattern further, start with the [masking inventory](/resources/masking-inventory). If the results feel significant or familiar, the next step may be a conversation with a qualified clinician through our [new client process](/resources/new-client).
