## What an autism evaluation involves

An autism evaluation at Twilight Psychology typically includes a combination of:

- **Clinical interview** — developmental history, current concerns, how symptoms present across different settings
- **Direct observation and structured assessment** — including standardized tools such as the **ADOS-2** (Autism Diagnostic Observation Schedule), which is considered one of the gold-standard autism assessment instruments
- **Rating scales and collateral questionnaires** — from parents, caregivers, partners, or teachers when appropriate
- **Cognitive and adaptive testing** — to understand the person's broader profile, including strengths and areas of difficulty
- **Feedback session** — a conversation reviewing findings in plain language, along with written recommendations

Not every evaluation uses the same combination of measures. The battery is shaped by the person's age, the referral question, and what the clinical picture suggests is most relevant. For adults and for people with high-masking presentations, that flexibility is especially important.

## Why autism evaluation for adults looks different

Autism in adults — and particularly in women, nonbinary individuals, and people who developed strong coping strategies early — often presents differently than the childhood presentations that traditional screening tools were designed to capture.

Many adults arrive at an autism evaluation after years of managing social demands, sensory experiences, and executive function challenges in ways that look "functional" from the outside. Internally, that management often comes at significant cost. This is sometimes called **masking or camouflaging** — fitting in by suppressing autistic traits, mirroring others' behavior, or developing elaborate rules for navigating social situations.

Standard ADOS-2 protocols and behavioral questionnaires were developed primarily with male children in mind, which means they can miss high-masking adults — particularly women — even when autism is genuinely present. At Twilight Psychology, our evaluators are experienced with high-masking presentations and supplement standardized tools with clinical judgment and collateral information to produce a more accurate picture.

If masking is part of what you are trying to understand, our post on [the CAT-Q masking tool](/blog/clinical/how-to-understand-your-cat-q-masking-results) provides helpful background on what masking looks like and how it is assessed.

## Children and adolescent autism evaluations

For children, autism evaluation often comes after a school flag, a pediatric referral, or a parent's growing sense that something is not quite adding up — socially, behaviorally, or developmentally. The evaluation process for children includes caregiver interview and developmental history as a central component, because early developmental patterns matter significantly to how autism presents and whether a diagnosis fits.

For younger children, autism evaluation at Twilight can begin **as young as age 2**, depending on the referral question and clinician match. Early evaluation does not lock a child into a permanent label — it helps families understand what is going on now, access appropriate services sooner, and plan more effectively.

For older children and teenagers, the evaluation also considers how a young person understands themselves, whether they have developed masking patterns, and how the diagnostic picture affects their identity and support needs.

## Autism and ADHD overlap — AuDHD

Autism and ADHD co-occur more often than many people expect. The combination — sometimes referred to as **AuDHD** — can be harder to identify because symptoms of one condition can mask or amplify symptoms of the other. Impulsivity that looks like ADHD may have an autistic driver. Attention difficulties that look like ADHD may relate more to sensory overwhelm or executive function differences tied to autism.

An evaluation that looks at both — rather than forcing a single-diagnosis frame — produces a more accurate clinical picture and more useful recommendations. Several of our clinicians specialize specifically in this overlap.

## PDA — a profile that is often missed

Pathological Demand Avoidance (PDA) is a profile on the autism spectrum characterized by an intense anxiety-driven need to avoid everyday demands. It is frequently missed in standard evaluations because people with PDA profiles often appear verbally fluent and socially engaged in ways that traditional autism screening tools do not flag.

If demand avoidance — not defiance, but an anxiety-driven, autonomy-driven pattern — is part of what you are trying to understand, our post on [what a PDA profile is and how we assess it](/blog/clinical/what-is-pda-profile) covers this in more depth.

## What a diagnosis does and does not do

An autism diagnosis does not change who a person is. It gives language to something that was already true.

For many people — especially adults who have spent years wondering — a diagnosis provides relief, clarity, and access to support that was not previously available. It can open doors to workplace accommodations, therapy that fits, community, and a different internal narrative about past struggles.

For children, a diagnosis is often the gateway to school accommodations, therapeutic services, and a support plan that accounts for how the child actually processes the world.

What the evaluation ends with — beyond a diagnostic conclusion — is a set of **written recommendations** and a feedback session that translates the findings into practical next steps.
